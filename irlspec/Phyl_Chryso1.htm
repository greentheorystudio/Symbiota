Division Bacillariophyta

Diatom Glossary

DIATOMS

Many microalgal groups, including diatoms, are in a state of taxonomic flux due to advances in optical science and, particularly, genetic analysis. Whereas in the 19th century diatoms were considered to consist of one family, by the mid-20th century they were assumed to be a single class, Bacillariophyceae, with two orders, Centrales and Pennales.

In 1990, the benchmark book ‘The Diatoms’ (Round et al. 1990) proposed three classes (Coscinodiscophyceae, Fragilariophyceae and Bacillariophyceae) with 11 subclasses and 45 orders. Based on a combination of morphology, ultrastructure, life history details and molecular genetics, Medlin and Kaczmarska (2004) emended the Coscinodiscophyceae and Bacillariophyceae of Round et al. (1990), and established a new class, Mediophyceae, which they proposed as phylogenetically based. This taxonomic arrangement has not been generally accepted as yet (Williams & Kociolek 2007, 2011; rebuttal by Medlin 2010; Theriot et al. 2009). One of the more controversial changes is the transfer of the order Thalassiosirales from Coscinodiscophyceae to Mediophyceae.

For the sake of organization, the diatoms are grouped in this inventory alphabetically within the classes following Medlin and Kaczmarska (2004), with no implications for strict phylogenetic relationships, and exclusive of order or family organization for which there is continual and considerable controversy.A substantial number of genera apportioned in these classes have yet to be analyzed by molecular genetic techniques, so their placement in classes here is based on their morphological affinities.

General Morphology Of a Diatom Cell

The entire diatom cell is called a frustule, with a cell wall of hydrated silicon dioxide (i.e. glass). In its simplest form, it has three main components: two valves and a girdle, also called the cingulum. The two valves are called the epivalve and the hypovalve.

Between the two valves, in most cases, are a varying number of partially or wholly circumferential siliceous bands, which combine to form the girdle or cingulum (G). If there are multiple girdle bands, the one closest to the valve is called the valvocopula; the other bands are called simply copulae, intercalary bands, or pleurae. The epivalve and its associated bands (Se + Ge) are called the epitheca (E); the hypovalve and its associated bands (Sh +Gh) are the hypotheca (H). The epitheca overlaps the hypotheca. The upper surface of a valve (S) is often called a valve ‘face’; the sloping surface leading to the girdle bands is the mantle (m). The transition from valve face to mantle may be gradual or abrupt.

In the microscope, diatom cells may be seen, depending on their dimensions, either from the top (‘valve view’) or from the side (‘girdle view’). In a cell that is radially symmetrical (Figure 5A, so-called ‘centric’ diatoms), the width of a cell is the diameter (D). The distance from the epitheca surface through the frustule to the hypotheca surface is the pervalvar axis (P).

In a cell that is bilaterally symmetrical (Figure 5B, so-called ‘pennate’ diatoms), its length is called the apical axis (A) and the width is the transapical axis (T). Detailed descriptions of diatom cell morphology are found in Round et al. (1990) and Hasle & Syvertsen (1997).
