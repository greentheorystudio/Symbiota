Australian Spotted Jellyfish Makes Its First Appearance in the IRL

The Australian spotted jellyfish, Phyllorhiza punctata, made its first documented appearance in the northern Indian River Lagoon in late June. Biologists Lauren Hall and Shelly Heidelbaugh of the St. Johns River Water Management District observed 2 specimens while surveying seagrass beds in Brevard County.   One of the specimens was collected for identification and further examination. A subsequent aerial survey over north Indian River and Banana River was conducted in the first week of July to estimate population centers and the number of individuals present. Approximately 2 dozen animals were sighted during the flight, leading researchers to estimate the Phyllorhiza population at that time numbered between 300 - 500 individuals. Animals were concentrated on both sides of Merrit Island, between the Eau Gallie Causeway and State Road 520. None of the animals was observed south of Melbourne Beach.

Phyllorhiza punctata is native to the waters off Australia, and is considered invasive in the United States. The species was first documented in parts of the Caribbean some 30 years ago, where non-explosive populations became established and still persist today. Phyllorhiza first appeared in the Gulf of Mexico in June of 2000 along the coasts of Louisiana, Mississippi, and Alabama. First spotted in Mobile Bay in June, by late summer, commercial fishermen were reporting shrimp nets clogged with spotted jellies within a 95 square-mile area of the Gulf. Dr. Monty Graham of the Dauphin Island Sea Lab in Alabama reported that aerial surveys of the Gulf population revealed dense aggregations of Phyllorhiza measuring approximately 100 m across, and as much as 1 kilometer in length. Graham estimated that some of these aggregations contained as many as 50 individuals per square meter. However, Phyllorhiza blooms are largely seasonal in nature, and by later September, the Gulf bloom had dissipated.

Spotted jellyfish, like their native counterparts in Florida, are voracious eaters that consume small fish eggs, larvae, and other microzooplankton. Thus, their appearance in the Gulf of Mexico caused some concern among ecologists and members of the general public, who feared that an established population of Phyllorhiza could be potentially damaging to fish and shrimp stocks. Fortunately, the population explosion of last summer was not repeated this summer; though some animals are present, their numbers are significantly reduced. Researchers speculate that environmental conditions in the Gulf may not have been optimal for Phyllorhiza to establish a viable, self-sustaining population.  

There is some reason to speculate that the Indian River Lagoon may be a somewhat more hospitable habitat for Phyllorhiza than the Gulf

Spotted jellies in the waters off Australia, and those that have become naturalized in the Caribbean, generally have zooxanthellae, a type of symbiotic algae, resident within their tissues. While none of the specimens collected in the Gulf of Mexico last summer were found to have zooxanthellae, the few specimens examined from the IRL did. However, the population explosion observed in the Gulf of Mexico last year has not been repeated in the Indian River Lagoon this year. Rather, sighting reports have dropped off significantly according to the St. Johns River Water Management District which had set up a hotline (1-800-226-3747) for Phyllorhiza sightings.

The question which causes the most concern for researchers is whether or not the spotted jellyfish will be able to establish a resident population in the Indian River Lagoon. The occurrence of this potentially damaging invasive species will certainly warrant further monitoring throughout the next few summers; but for this summer, the occurrence of Phyllorhiza punctata along Florida's east coast can be viewed as an ecological curiosity rather than an ecological disaster.
